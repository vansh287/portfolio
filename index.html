Abstract

I am a mathematics undergraduate (minor: data science) who originally wanted to study biology — and who found, after a long detour through theorems and code, a way back to the body: teaching computers to read medical images. My interest is computer vision for medical imaging — representation learning, generalization, novelty detection: the unglamorous, essential work of making a model behave when it meets data it has never seen. I learned statistics by implementing it from scratch at IIT Madras, fell for vision while chasing unknown objects through street scenes, and am currently being humbled by raw ultrasound signals (TU Dresden) and by PET/CT scans of head-and-neck cancer (IIT Jammu, MICCAI 2026 HECKTOR). What follows is a snapshot of that trajectory. This document is under active revision — and so am I.

Keywords: medical image analysis · radiomics · novelty detection · Bayesian inference · reproducibility

§ 1

Introduction

I work on computer vision for medical imaging — building models that are accurate, interpretable, and grounded in the mathematics underneath, not just the benchmark on top. What follows is how someone from the mountains of Kashmir found his way to that.

I grew up in Kashmir, surrounded by mountains that take instructions from no one. Maybe that's where it started: I knew early that I didn't want a life spent executing someone else's ideas on someone else's schedule — I wanted to develop my own. Research was the only job description that matched. So I cleared the IISER Aptitude Test and moved nearly the entire length of India south, to study by the Arabian Sea.

The original plan, honestly, was biology — but my Class XII stream had other opinions. So I took the next door over: a BS-MS in mathematics with a minor in data science. Rigorous enough to go anywhere; flexible enough, I hoped, to find my way back to living things eventually. (Spoiler: it worked. Medical imaging is biology — you just enter through the mathematics wing.)

I refused to commit to a field I hadn't tested, so I treated my early years as a deliberate survey of AI and ML. First a summer at IIT Madras doing statistical learning — implementing models from scratch, learning what real data does to clean theory. Then a semester of semantic segmentation back at IISER TVM, and that project decided everything: for the first time I could literally see what my mathematics was doing — masks, boundaries, objects assembling themselves out of pixels. Equations I could watch. I was gone.

Then I discovered that computer vision and medicine overlap, and the two halves of the plan — the biology I wanted and the mathematics I trained — finally clicked together: ultrasound radiomics with TU Dresden, PET/CT oncology pipelines with IIT Jammu. Since then there is no going back. (Other corners of computer vision remain very much on the exploration list.)

Research interests

Broadly, I want computer-vision systems that earn a clinician's trust: robust before they are clever, interpretable before they are state-of-the-art. Whether a model points at the right thing matters to me more than where it lands on a leaderboard.

Currently drawn to: medical image segmentation · radiomics & quantitative imaging · representation & self-supervised learning · 3D / volumetric vision · the geometry and statistics underneath it all.

§ 2

What I'm building now

2.1Teaching one pipeline three clinical questions

in progress IIT Jammu · research intern Jun 2026 → present · hybrid MICCAI 2026 · HECKTOR

For the MICCAI 2026 HECKTOR challenge I'm building a single, clean multimodal pipeline that reads FDG-PET/CT scans of head-and-neck cancer and answers three clinically linked questions: where is the disease (automatic segmentation of primary tumours and lymph nodes), how advanced is it (radiological TN staging), and what happens next (recurrence-free survival prognosis).

  • One pipeline, three heads — segmentation, staging and prognosis share one backbone instead of living in three scripts.
  • Radiomics-inspired texture features with robust normalization, so results survive the jump between hospitals and scanners.
  • Docker-packaged and modular, because "works on my machine" is not a clinical standard.

2.2Reading ultrasound before it becomes a picture

in progress TU Dresden . Germany Jan 2026 → present

In the QuantUS open-source project I develop the core B-mode radiomics pipeline (QuantUS-Backend-QUS): first-order and GLCM texture features computed directly from raw RF ultrasound signals — before the familiar grey picture even exists. The interesting problem is trust: an ultrasound number should mean the same thing on every machine, so I implement phantom-based normalization to make results device-independent and reproducible. I also work on backend refactoring and GUI integration, because a method only matters if a clinician can actually click on it.

§ 3

Earlier work

3.1Segmenting things no dataset warned you about

IISER TVM Aug 2025 → Dec 2025

Streets are full of objects no training set anticipated — and a vision system that only recognises what it was shown is a liability. I built an automated pipeline for semantic segmentation of both known and novel objects, extending ideas from AIDE with lightweight captioning models (GLAMM, CODA-LM) so the system can describe what it cannot yet classify. Proof-of-concept on Cityscapes; benchmarked on IDD, IDD-Anomaly and Carla-Anomaly.

3.2Rebuilding machine learning before trusting it

IIT Madras May 2025 → Jul 2025

Before I let libraries do my thinking, I spent a summer implementing classical and Bayesian machine-learning models from scratch — NumPy, first principles, no shortcuts — and comparing them honestly against their scikit-learn counterparts on real datasets. Regression, classification, the full report. It permanently changed how I read other people's models.
